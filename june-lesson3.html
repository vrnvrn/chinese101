二 vs 两: 二 (èr) is the counting number — dates, phone numbers, "the second". 两 (liǎng) means "two of something" and always rides in front of a measure word: 两个人 (not 二个人), 两杯茶.

Building Bigger Numbers

十 is also a Lego brick.

Chinese numbers are stackable. 十 builds the teens and tens; 百 and 千 take you up from there. No new grammar — just read left to right.

Hundreds & thousands
一百 · 三百 · 一千 · 三千
yìbǎi · sānbǎi · yìqiān · sānqiān

百 (bǎi) = hundred, 千 (qiān) = thousand. Prices live here: 这本书一百块 · 这个手机三千块.

Halves & Order

半, 一半, 第.

Three small but high-frequency number helpers: a half inside a count, a half on its own, and the prefix that turns numbers into "the -th".

半
bàn · half (within a count)
Sits after the measure word.
现在五点半。
It's 5:30 now.
一半
yíbàn · one half (on its own)
Stands alone as "half of it".
我喝了一半。
I drank half of it.
第
dì · ordinal prefix
第 + number = "the -th".
他是第一名。
He's first place.

半 goes after the measure word (五点半 = half past five, 一个半 = one and a half). 第 goes before the number (第一 = first, 第三 = third). Don't mix the slots.

02
Measure Words
个 · 本 · 件 · 块 · 口 · 只 · 元 — the counters between number and noun
The One Rule

You can't count a noun directly.

In English you say "three books". In Chinese you must wedge a measure word in between: number → measure word → noun. Never 三书; always 三本书.

Number + 个 + Noun · the default
我有一个问题。
Wǒ yǒu yí gè wèntí.
I have a question.
Wrong — no measure word
三✗书
sān shū
"three book" — ungrammatical

个 (gè) is the all-purpose measure word from Lesson 2. When you don't know the right one, 个 is almost always understood. The next pages take the measure words one at a time — what each one counts, and a few sentences.

Measure Word · 1 of 8

个 — the all-purpose one.

The default measure word. When you're not sure which one a noun takes, reach for 个 and you'll almost always be understood.

个
gè
people, things, and most abstract nouns
Connects to
人rén · person 朋友péngyou · friend 孩子háizi · child 问题wèntí · question
In sentences
我有一个问题。
Wǒ yǒu yí gè wèntí.
I have a question.
这个人是我朋友。
Zhège rén shì wǒ péngyou.
This person is my friend.
我要两个。
Wǒ yào liǎng gè.
I'll take two.

In speech 个 usually drops to a neutral tone — you'll hear yí ge, not yí gè. Don't let that throw you.

Measure Word · 2 of 8

本 — for books.

Anything bound with pages you can flip through: books, magazines, dictionaries, notebooks.

本
běn
bound, printed things
Connects to
书shū · book 杂志zázhì · magazine 词典cídiǎn · dictionary
In sentences
这本书很好看。
Zhè běn shū hěn hǎokàn.
This book is great.
我有三本书。
Wǒ yǒu sān běn shū.
I have three books.

Rule of thumb: if it has pages you can turn, it takes 本.

Measure Word · 3 of 8

件 — for clothes & matters.

Two jobs: items of upper-body clothing, and "matters / pieces of business" you have to deal with.

件
jiàn
clothing items · affairs
Connects to
衣服yīfu · clothes 事shì · matter 事情shìqing · affair
In sentences
这件衣服很漂亮。
Zhè jiàn yīfu hěn piàoliang.
This piece of clothing is pretty.
我有一件事。
Wǒ yǒu yí jiàn shì.
I have something to discuss.

件 covers things you wear on top (shirts, coats) — but not trousers or shoes, which have their own measure words.

Measure Word · 4 of 8

块 — for money & chunks.

The everyday spoken word for money ("bucks"), and the counter for lumps or pieces of something.

块
kuài
yuan (spoken) · pieces, lumps
Connects to
钱qián · money 蛋糕dàngāo · cake 肉ròu · meat
In sentences
一杯茶五块钱。
Yì bēi chá wǔ kuài qián.
A cup of tea is 5 yuan.
这个十块。
Zhège shí kuài.
This one is ten yuan.

块 is what you actually say when paying. Its formal written twin is 元 — two pages from now.

Measure Word · 5 of 8

口 — for family members.

Literally "mouth". Used almost only to count how many people are in a household.

口
kǒu
people in a family
Connects to
人rén · person (in a family) 家jiā · home, family
In sentences
我家有四口人。
Wǒ jiā yǒu sì kǒu rén.
My family has four people.
你家有几口人？
Nǐ jiā yǒu jǐ kǒu rén?
How many people are in your family?

口 is special to family size. For people in general (friends, students, strangers), go back to 个.

Measure Word · 6 of 8

只 — for animals.

The go-to counter for most small animals — pets, birds, and the like.

只
zhī
animals
Connects to
猫māo · cat 狗gǒu · dog 鸟niǎo · bird
In sentences
我有一只猫。
Wǒ yǒu yì zhī māo.
I have one cat.
我家有两只狗。
Wǒ jiā yǒu liǎng zhī gǒu.
My family has two dogs.

Most small animals take 只. A few big ones (horses, cattle) have their own — but 只 will get you understood early on.

Measure Word · 7 of 8

元 — for money, formally.

The same money as 块, but the written, formal form. You'll see it on price tags, receipts, and signs.

元
yuán
yuan (written / formal)
Connects to
钱qián · money 价钱jiàqian · price
In sentences
这个面包十元。
Zhège miànbāo shí yuán.
This bread is 10 yuan.
一共一百元。
Yígòng yìbǎi yuán.
A hundred yuan in total.

块 vs 元: same value, different register. You say 块 and read 元 — like "bucks" vs "dollars" on a receipt.

Measure Word · 8 of 8

些 — for "some", no number.

A measure word for a vague plural — "some / a few". You met it inside 这些 / 那些 in Lesson 2; on its own it rides behind 一 or a pointer.

些
xiē
an unspecified handful
Connects to
书shū · books 朋友péngyou · friends 东西dōngxi · things
In sentences
桌子上有些书。
Zhuōzi shàng yǒu xiē shū.
There are some books on the table.
我有一些朋友。
Wǒ yǒu yìxiē péngyou.
I have some friends.
这些都是新的。
Zhèxiē dōu shì xīn de.
These are all new.

些 is itself a measure word, so never stack another on top: 一些书 ✓, never 一些个书. Think "a handful", not an exact count.

03
A Little of This
一点儿 vs 有点儿 — the two "a little"s that learners swap by mistake
The Classic Trap

一点儿 vs 有点儿.

Both translate as "a little", but they sit in different slots and carry different moods. Get this pair right and you sound far more natural.

一点儿 · neutral amount → before a noun / after a verb
我会一点儿汉语。
Wǒ huì yìdiǎnr Hànyǔ.
I know a little Chinese.
有点儿 · mild complaint → before an adjective
今天有点儿冷。
Jīntiān yǒudiǎnr lěng.
Today is a little (too) cold.
一点儿
Quantity word. Neutral. "a little (of something)". Comes after the verb or before the noun.
有点儿
Adverb. Slightly negative — "a bit too…". Comes before the adjective.

Quick test: 有点儿贵 = "a bit (too) expensive" (complaint). 便宜一点儿 = "a little cheaper" (an amount). If there's a grumble, it's 有点儿.
